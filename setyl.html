How setyl. works
Category 01 — Affordability

Affordability

The ratio of median home value to median household income. A ratio of 3.0 means the median home costs 3× the median annual income — a widely-used rule of thumb for "affordable." A ratio above 5 is considered severely unaffordable.

// ratio = median home value ÷ median income ratio ≤ 2.5 → 95 (very affordable) ratio ≤ 3.0 → 87 ratio ≤ 3.5 → 78 ratio ≤ 4.5 → 65 ratio ≤ 5.5 → 52 ratio ≤ 7.0 → 38 ratio > 7.0 → 25 (severely unaffordable)

Census ACS

Source: US Census Bureau — American Community Survey 5-Year Estimates (2022)

B25077_001E — Median home value  ·  B19013_001E — Median household income

ACS 5-Year Estimates · 2022 release

Covers 2018–2022. The Census Bureau releases new 5-year estimates each December. State and county pre-scored data was compiled in early 2025.

Category 02 — Safety

Safety

The violent crime rate per 100,000 residents in a county. Violent crime includes murder, rape, robbery, and aggravated assault as defined by the FBI UCR program.

// rate = violent crimes ÷ population × 100,000 // US national avg ≈ 380/100k rate < 100 → 99 (very safe) rate < 200 → 90 rate < 300 → 80 rate < 400 → 70 rate < 500 → 60 rate < 650 → 48 rate < 800 → 36 rate ≥ 800 → 24 (high crime)

At the town level, the county score is nudged ±4 pts by local median income as a within-county proxy.

FBI CIUS

Source: FBI Crime in the United States — Table 10: Offenses Known to Law Enforcement by County, 2024 release. Covers 2,416 counties.

Population denominators: Census ACS B01003_001E.

FBI CIUS Table 10 · 2024 release

Covers calendar year 2023 incidents. Published annually in fall of the following year. Loaded from a pre-processed snapshot (the FBI's public API was taken offline in early 2025).

Not all agencies report to the FBI. About 40% of counties have missing or incomplete data, especially rural areas. A reported value of 0 is treated as "no data" not "zero crime" — safety is excluded from that area's composite score rather than assumed safe.

Category 03 — Schools

Schools

The concentration of Title I public schools in a county. Title I designates schools where 40%+ of students qualify for free or reduced-price lunch. K–12 only — no private schools or colleges.

Higher score = fewer Title I schools = lower poverty concentration in schools.

// ratio = Title I schools ÷ total schools in county score = 95 − (ratio × 55) if magnet schools exist: score += 5 score = clamp(score, 20, 99) // Town-level BA degree attainment adjustment BA rate > 35% → +8 pts BA rate > 20% → +4 pts BA rate > 10% → 0 pts BA rate ≤ 10% → −5 pts

NCES CCD

Source: National Center for Education Statistics — Common Core of Data, accessed live via the Urban Institute Education Data API. Covers ~130,000 public K–12 schools.

Important: This is a socioeconomic proxy, not a direct quality measure.

We don't measure test scores, graduation rates, student-teacher ratios, AP availability, or state report card grades. A wealthy suburb with mediocre schools scores the same as one with excellent schools if Title I concentration is equal.

The score is also calculated at the county level — individual school districts within a county are not differentiated.

NCES CCD · 2021–22 school year · fetched live

School data is fetched fresh from the Urban Institute API each time you drill into a county. It is not cached between sessions.

Overall Score

Overall Score

// Weighted average of available categories overall = (housing × 0.45 + safety × 0.25 + schools × 0.30) ÷ sum_of_weights_for_available_data // If safety is NULL, weights rebalance: overall = (housing × 0.45 + schools × 0.30) ÷ 0.75 // If schools also NULL: overall = housing score only

NULL scores are excluded — they don't drag the composite down or get treated as zero.

Affordability (45%) uses precise Census figures with a clear, universal formula — it's the most directly measurable category and the primary intent of the app.

Safety (25%) and Schools (30%) use proxy measures and their importance varies significantly by life stage — a single renter weighs them differently than a family with school-age children.

Limitations

What This Doesn't Tell You

Town-level safety and schools are county-level data applied uniformly. Every town in a county shares the same base score, adjusted only slightly by local income and education attainment.

Crime data has gaps. ~40% of counties have incomplete FBI data. These show NULL, not zero.

ACS data is a survey estimate. For small towns (<5,000 people), margins of error can be large.

No walkability, jobs, or climate data. Commute times, job markets, weather, disaster risk, and cost of living beyond housing are not included.
